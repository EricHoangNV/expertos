# Lean Six Sigma Insights by Govind Ramu

- **Evolution of Lean Six Sigma**: 
  - Initially focused on tools (e.g., 5S, Kanban, Just-in-Time) for short-term benefits.
  - Lean emphasized effectiveness (goal achievement), while Six Sigma focused on efficiency (variation reduction).
  - Early implementations were siloed; Lean and Six Sigma teams operated separately.

- **Integration of Frameworks**:
  - Effective Lean requires a foundational Quality Management System (QMS).
  - The relationship between effectiveness (Lean) and efficiency (Six Sigma) is crucial; effectiveness must precede efficiency.
  - The framework evolved into a pyramid: QMS at the base, followed by Lean, then Six Sigma.

- **Current Focus**:
  - Shift from tool-based approaches to organizational learning, wisdom, and cultural alignment.
  - Emphasis on resilience and adaptability in a VUCA (Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, Ambiguous) world.
  - Organizations must be nimble and robust against external variables (e.g., geopolitical issues, environmental impacts).

- **Investment Justification**:
  - Lean Six Sigma (or similar improvement initiatives) is essential for organizations to innovate faster, reduce operational failures, and maintain quality.
  - The need for agility and resilience in business operations is heightened by recent global challenges (e.g., pandemics, wars).

- **Common Failures in Lean Six Sigma Initiatives**:
  - Lack of leadership support is a primary reason for failure.
  - Stealth implementations at lower levels without broader organizational buy-in can lead to loss of momentum.
  - Other nuanced failures include insufficient training, lack of clear goals, and failure to integrate into the organizational culture.

- **Key Takeaway**: 
  - The foundational principles of Lean Six Sigma remain unchanged, but their application must evolve to meet contemporary challenges, focusing on speed, resilience, and adaptability.
