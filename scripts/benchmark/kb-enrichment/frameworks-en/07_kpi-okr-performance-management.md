# KPI/OKR Performance Management and Red-KPI Recovery

Performance management turns strategy and process into tracked, owned outcomes. Done well it
drives accountability and improvement; done poorly it drives gaming, blame, and busywork.

## Leading vs lagging indicators
- **Lagging indicators** measure results after the fact (revenue, defect rate, on-time delivery).
  They confirm outcomes but cannot be acted on directly.
- **Leading indicators** measure the drivers that predict those results (pipeline coverage,
  first-pass yield, training completion, cycle time). They can be managed now to change the
  lagging outcome later.
A good KPI system pairs each critical outcome with the leading drivers that move it, so teams
manage causes, not just watch results. This is the outcome-to-driver logic: work backward from
the outcome you want to the drivers you can control.

## Meaningful KPIs
- Tie to strategy and customer value, not vanity metrics.
- Have a single accountable owner and a target with a basis (not an arbitrary aspiration).
- Balance dimensions so optimizing one does not wreck another (quality vs speed vs cost).
- Are visible on a cadence that drives decisions, not just reporting.

## Recovering a red KPI (a metric off target)
When a KPI goes red, resist two failure modes: ignoring it, or punishing the owner. Instead run
a disciplined recovery:
1. **Confirm the signal.** Is the miss a real special-cause shift or normal variation? (See SPC.)
2. **Diagnose with drivers.** Decompose the lagging KPI into its leading drivers and find which
   driver moved. Verify the root cause with data.
3. **Assign ownership and a countermeasure.** One owner, a PDCA countermeasure aimed at the
   verified driver, and a target recovery date.
4. **Review on cadence.** Track the leading drivers weekly until the lagging KPI recovers.
5. **Control.** Once recovered, standardize the fix and keep a leading-indicator control so it
   does not silently relapse.

## Run–Improve–Transform
Balance the performance portfolio across three horizons: **Run** (keep today's operations
stable and on target), **Improve** (raise the capability of existing processes), and
**Transform** (build new capabilities for the future). Managing only Run starves improvement;
chasing only Transform destabilizes the base. Explicitly allocate attention and resources across
all three.

## Targets and accountability
Targets should be evidence-based (grounded in process capability and benchmarks), and
accountability should sit with the person who owns the process that drives the metric — not with
whoever is nearest the number. Metric-to-manage only works when the metric maps to a controllable
process and a real owner.
