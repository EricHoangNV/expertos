# Accountability, RACI, and Decision Rights

Most "execution" and "alignment" problems are really accountability problems: it is unclear who
owns what, who decides, and who must be consulted. Clarifying accountability is often the highest-
leverage, lowest-cost intervention available.

## RACI
RACI assigns clear roles for each task or decision:
- **Responsible.** Does the work.
- **Accountable.** The single owner answerable for the outcome. There must be exactly one A per
  decision — two accountable owners means no owner.
- **Consulted.** Provides input before the decision (two-way).
- **Informed.** Told after the decision (one-way).
The two most common defects are **no single A** (diffuse ownership, so nothing moves) and **too
many C's** (everyone consulted, so decisions stall). Fixing these unblocks execution without any
new tools or headcount.

## Decision rights and speed
Slow organizations usually have unclear or over-centralized decision rights. Speed comes from
placing each decision at the lowest level that has the information and competence to make it, with
clear boundaries. Define, for each significant decision type: who decides, who must be consulted,
what the escalation trigger is, and what the decision-maker is empowered to spend or change.
Clear decision rights convert into speed; ambiguous ones convert into meetings.

## Dissent to decision
Healthy decisions invite dissent before the decision and demand commitment after it. Use a
structured process: surface disagreement and evidence openly, let the accountable owner decide,
then everyone commits and executes ("disagree and commit"). Endless re-litigation after a decision
is an ownership and process failure.

## Meetings to decisions
Meetings should produce decisions and owned actions, not just discussion. A good operating cadence
turns each review into: what changed, what decision is needed, who owns the action, by when. If a
meeting produces no decision and no owned action, its purpose and attendee list should be
questioned.

## System-before-person
Before holding a person accountable for a poor outcome, confirm they actually own the system that
produces it and have the authority to change it. Holding people accountable for outcomes they
cannot control destroys trust and drives gaming; align accountability with real control.
