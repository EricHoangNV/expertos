# Structured Problem Solving: PDCA and DMAIC

Disciplined improvement uses a structured method so problems are actually solved and stay
solved, rather than patched. The two foundational cycles are PDCA and DMAIC.

## PDCA (Plan–Do–Check–Act)
The core improvement and management loop:
- **Plan.** Define the problem with data, set a target, analyze causes, and design a
  countermeasure.
- **Do.** Pilot the countermeasure on a small scale.
- **Check.** Compare results against the target using data — did it work, and why?
- **Act.** If it worked, standardize it (update the SOP) and roll out; if not, adjust and cycle
  again.
PDCA is also the engine of management review cadence: each review runs Check and Act on the
priorities.

## DMAIC (Define–Measure–Analyze–Improve–Control)
The Six Sigma project method for reducing defects and variation:
- **Define.** Scope the problem, the customer requirement (CTQ), and the goal.
- **Measure.** Establish a reliable baseline; validate the measurement system; quantify current
  performance and variation.
- **Analyze.** Find and **verify** the root cause with data (not opinion) — hypothesis testing,
  Pareto, process analysis.
- **Improve.** Design, pilot, and confirm a solution that addresses the verified root cause.
- **Control.** Lock in the gain: standardize, put controls/SPC in place, assign ownership, and
  monitor so the problem cannot silently return.

## The discipline that matters
- **Problem before solution.** Do not jump to a countermeasure before the problem and its root
  cause are defined and verified. Most failed improvements skip Define/Measure/Analyze.
- **Measure before improve.** Without a validated baseline you cannot know whether a change
  helped.
- **Improve to sustain.** A fix without the Control/Act step is temporary; the gain erodes.
  Standardization plus a control mechanism is what makes improvement permanent.

## When to use which
Use PDCA for continuous, everyday improvement and management cadence. Use DMAIC for larger,
data-intensive problems where variation and defect reduction need statistical rigor. Both share
the same spine: define and verify before you act, and control before you close.
